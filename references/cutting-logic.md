# Cutting Logic

Use this reference for multi-shot sequences, edit points, shot-to-shot continuity, attention debt, axis, reaction shots, omission, montage, and cut diagnosis.

## Definition

A cut is not “changing angle.”

A cut is the next shot's treatment of a visual/audio signal, information gap, action tendency, spatial relation, or emotional pressure established by the previous shot.

Basic structure:

`previous shot tail signal -> audience attention expectation -> next shot treatment`

Every effective cut answers:

1. What did the previous shot push audience attention toward?
2. What does the audience naturally expect to see, know, or feel now?
3. Does the next shot follow, delay, interrupt, reverse, or transfer that expectation?

If these questions cannot be answered, the cut is weak.

## Highest Principles

### Cut Motivation

Valid motivations:

- direction
- causality
- action
- sound
- gaze
- space change
- time change
- emotional change
- information reveal
- rhythm
- subjective/objective switch
- scale switch
- contrast
- obstruction
- narrative omission
- attention transfer

Invalid motivations:

- “I want another angle.”
- “Put a close-up here.”
- “Make the image richer.”
- “It feels like we should cut.”

### Attention Debt

When a shot creates a signal, it creates attention debt.

Signals include:

- someone looks offscreen
- someone reaches
- sound comes from offscreen
- light flashes from somewhere
- character pauses
- attack moves in a direction
- object is emphasized
- spatial exit is hinted
- character state is abnormal
- environment detail is emphasized

The next shot must process the debt. It does not always need to answer directly, but must acknowledge it.

### Follow, Delay, Reverse, Never Ignore

Effective cuts:

- **Follow**: directly respond to the attention direction.
- **Delay**: postpone response while giving related information.
- **Reverse**: break expectation with higher-priority truth.

Ineffective cut:

- **Unrelated**: abandons previous signal without continuation, explanation, transfer, or reversal.

## Cut Operators

### 1. Continuation

Previous shot establishes a signal; next shot catches it.

Objects:

- gaze object
- speech target
- sound source
- action target
- motion direction
- force result
- danger source
- information source
- emotional object
- spatial exit
- light source direction
- area behind obstruction

### 2. Progression

Previous shot establishes a state; next shot changes it.

Progression may be distance, action, conflict, information, emotion, danger, space, time, physical process, or decision.

### 3. Result

Previous shot gives cause or action; next shot gives result.

Result can be physical, human, environmental, prop, sound, light, emotion, information, or spatial.

Useful for omitting complex process while preserving cause.

### 4. Reaction

Previous shot gives event; next shot gives affected person/object/environment response.

Reaction is not “shooting a face.” It makes an event have visible consequence.

### 5. Reorientation

Use when spatial relation may be unclear:

- after movement
- after scene change
- after multiple close-ups
- after possible axis confusion
- after new danger source
- after distance change
- after perspective switch
- after complex obstruction
- when battle area expands or contracts

Reorientation restores the audience's spatial map.

### 6. Omission

Previous shot gives action start; next shot skips process and gives mid-state, result, or residue.

Useful when:

- process is unimportant
- process is too complex
- model cannot generate it
- rhythm needs compression
- speed should be felt
- key action should be hidden
- audience should infer backward
- mystery should remain

Omission must leave enough causal evidence.

### 7. Delay

Previous shot creates a question; next shot gives related information before answer.

Use for suspense, threat, reversal, ability reveal, danger approach, character judgment, information puzzle, rhythm control, emotional pressure.

Delay has an upper limit. Keep providing relevant evidence.

### 8. Reversal

Previous shot builds expectation; next shot overturns it.

Reversal can involve direction, identity, danger source, target, intent, space, emotion, power, time, or explanation.

It must reveal higher-level truth, not random surprise.

### 9. Juxtaposition

Previous and next shot compare rather than cause.

Compare strong/weak, near/far, fast/slow, still/moving, inside/outside, subjective/objective, person/environment, action/consequence, appearance/truth, individual/group, detail/scale.

### 10. Transfer

Attention moves from A to B.

Valid only if B has spatial, causal, informational, emotional, rhythmic, or priority relation to A.

### 11. Subjectivization

Move from objective view into a character's filter:

- sight
- hearing
- breath
- pain
- dizziness
- fear
- judgment
- attention focus
- ability perception
- device readout
- memory residue

It is not internal monologue; it changes information filtering.

### 12. Objectivization

Pull back from local/subjective perception to objective space.

Use to remove misdirection, restore space, show true scale, reveal information unknown to the character, or ground subjective pressure in environment.

### 13. Scale Switch

Macro to micro, micro to macro, whole to part, part to whole, human scale to giant scale, space scale to body scale, environment scale to prop scale, battlefield scale to contact point.

Must serve information.

### 14. Time Switch

Continuous time, compressed time, omitted time, parallel time, flashback, foreshadowing, repetition, slow motion, jump cut, delayed time, action replay.

If shots are continuous, state time relation clearly.

### 15. Axis Maintenance

Maintain left/right, front/back, movement direction, attack direction, gaze direction, entrances/exits, distance, danger approach direction.

This is the base of continuity editing.

### 16. Axis Break

Only use for loss of control, spatial confusion, impact, subjective dizziness, terror collapse, position reversal, broken reality order, or intended temporary disorientation.

Rebuild space afterward.

### 17. Match

Connect via action, direction, shape, light, sound, color, speed, rhythm, composition, or physical trajectory.

Match is a transition tool, not a narrative reason by itself.

### 18. Designed Discontinuity

Use for scare, illusion, dream, memory, mental collapse, time jump, violent shock, information gap, absurdity, non-real experience.

Undesigned discontinuity is an error.

## Six Dimensions

Judge every cut through six dimensions:

1. **Information**: reveal, confirm, hide, correct, question, delay, refine, reduce certainty, local to whole, appearance to truth.
2. **Space**: position, direction, distance, depth, scale, axis, obstruction space, offscreen space, spatial map.
3. **Time**: continuous, skipped, compressed, delayed, parallel, repeated, faster, slower, offset, broken.
4. **Action/force**: preparation, load, release, contact, block, slide, stuck, feedback, imbalance, residue, recovery.
5. **Emotion/attention**: tension, release, doubt, fear, hope collapse, judgment shift, focus, diffusion, held emotion, eruption.
6. **Rhythm**: speed up, slow down, pause, burst, compress, stretch, off-beat, repeated cut, silence.

## Cut Formula

For each cut:

1. What signal did the previous shot establish?
2. What attention expectation did it create?
3. Which operator does the next shot use?
4. What change does the audience receive?
5. Does the change advance story, action, space, emotion, or information?

If no, delete the cut.

## Legality Check

A cut is valid if it does at least one:

- catches previous direction signal
- advances previous action or state
- answers previous question
- shows event result
- shows reaction
- re-establishes space
- changes information level
- changes time rhythm
- changes subjective/objective perspective
- establishes necessary comparison
- omits while preserving causal evidence
- creates designed discontinuity with narrative function

## Priority

1. **Continuity cuts**: direction, action, axis, reorientation, state inheritance, cause/result, reaction.
2. **Information cuts**: question/answer, reveal, delay, hide, mislead, confirm, reverse, juxtapose.
3. **Emotion cuts**: reaction, pause, delay, compression, silence, burst, subjectivization, objectivization.
4. **Style cuts**: match, designed discontinuity, shape transition, light transition, rhythmic off-beat.

Never let style cuts break continuity or information logic unless the break is itself designed and repaired.

## Error Types

- No-signal cut: previous shot has no signal needing response.
- Debt-lost cut: strong signal is abandoned.
- Direction-wrong cut: gaze, motion, attack, or sound direction reverses without explanation.
- Information-overload cut: both shots carry too much new information.
- Emotion-grab cut: emotional close-up before enough physical/information foundation.
- Style-grab cut: cool transition breaks action, direction, space, causality.
- Missing reaction cut: major action/info/emotion has no reaction or consequence.
- Missing reorientation cut: complex motion without spatial reset.
- Fake omission cut: skips process with no causal evidence.
- Fake reversal cut: breaks expectation without higher information support.
